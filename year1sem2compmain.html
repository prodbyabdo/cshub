Lecture 1 · Introduction to Maintenance
What is Maintenance?
Maintenance is the set of actions required to keep or restore a system, equipment, or facility to a specified operational condition so that it can perform its required functions.
  • Ensures reliability and availability of systems
  • Reduces unexpected failures and downtime
  • Extends the useful life of equipment
  • Protects investment and ensures safety

Lockout / Tagout (LOTO) Procedure

LOTO prevents unexpected energisation of equipment during maintenance. Follow this sequence strictly:

1
Identify energy sources — electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, chemical, thermal, gravity.
2
Notify affected workers — inform all personnel that equipment will be shut down.
3
Shut down equipment — follow the manufacturer's shutdown procedure.
4
Isolate energy sources — disconnect all power, close valves, block gravitational parts.
5
Apply lockout device — attach a physical lock to each isolation point.
6
Apply tagout tag — attach "DO NOT OPERATE" tag with date and technician name.
7
Release stored energy — bleed pressure, discharge capacitors, lower suspended loads.
8
Verify isolation — attempt to start equipment and test with a meter; confirm zero energy.

Lecture 3 · Types of Maintenance Strategies
Corrective Maintenance

Restore failed equipment to working condition after a breakdown occurs.

✓ Simple to apply   ✗ High downtime cost
Preventive Maintenance

Scheduled servicing at fixed intervals regardless of condition.

✓ Reduces surprises   ✗ May service working parts
Predictive Maintenance (PdM)

Monitor real-time indicators to predict failures before they occur.

✓ Highly efficient   ✗ High setup cost
Condition-Based (CBM)

Maintenance triggered by actual equipment condition, not schedule.

✓ Right-timed actions   ✗ Needs sensor investment
Breakdown Maintenance

Allow non-critical equipment to run until failure, then repair.

✓ Zero upfront cost   ✗ Unpredictable failures
Reliability-Centered (RCM)

Systematic analysis to determine optimal maintenance strategy per component.

✓ Best outcome   ✗ Complex analysis needed

Strategy Selection Factors

Criticality

Higher criticality → more proactive strategy (PdM or RCM).

Failure Consequences

Safety/production impact determines acceptable risk level.

Failure Frequency

Frequent failures → preventive; rare failures → corrective may suffice.

Cost of Downtime

High downtime cost justifies investment in proactive maintenance.

Monitorability

If condition can be measured reliably, PdM or CBM is preferred.

Budget & Resources

Available tools, sensors, skilled staff and maintenance budget.

Lecture 4 · Corrective & Predictive — Deep Dive
Corrective Maintenance Types

Immediate Corrective

Repair is performed right away to restore the system as quickly as possible. Used when failure has critical safety or operational impact.

  • Emergency response
  • High priority resources allocated
  • No delay tolerated

Deferred Corrective

Repair is planned and carried out at a convenient future time. Used when failure impact is low and a workaround exists.

  • Scheduled into maintenance window
  • Parts procured in advance
  • Lower urgency, lower cost
